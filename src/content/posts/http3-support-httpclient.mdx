JDK 26 brought native support for HTTP/3 to the standard `java.net.http.HttpClient`. HTTP/3 is built
on QUIC — a UDP-based protocol — rather than TCP, which resolves the head-of-line blocking issues
that plagued older protocols over unreliable networks.

## Architectural usage

If your microservices communicate across diverse geographic regions, or connect to external partner
APIs, HTTP/3 noticeably reduces connection setup latency and improves resilience against packet
loss. It's a largely seamless upgrade for inter-service communication speed if you're already on the
native `HttpClient` — most of the change is at the transport layer, not your call sites.

<div class="callout">
Before flipping this on for production inter-service traffic, check your network path: HTTP/3 relies
on UDP, and some corporate proxies, load balancers, or restrictive security groups block or
deprioritize UDP traffic by default. Validate the whole hop, not just the client and server.
</div>
